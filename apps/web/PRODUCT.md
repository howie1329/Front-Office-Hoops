# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Hardcore basketball simulation players and sports management sim players who want to run a league with credible front-office control. They are comfortable with dense information, standings, rosters, schedules, draft decisions, transactions, and season simulation controls. They come to the app to make decisions quickly, inspect league state, and understand the consequences of each move.

## Product Purpose

Front Office Hoops is a simulation-first basketball general manager product. It exists to let users create and manage leagues, pick a team, advance seasons, track standings and schedules, evaluate players, handle roster and financial decisions, and move through playoffs, draft, free agency, and league history. Success means the interface feels fast, trustworthy, and analytical enough that the simulation becomes the focus.

## Brand Personality

Analytical, fast, league-office.

The product should feel like a serious front-office tool for people who enjoy simulation depth. It should be compact, direct, and decision-oriented, with copy that favors clarity over hype.

## Anti-references

Do not make the app feel like a mobile game store UI, an ESPN-style news site, a fantasy-sports betting dashboard, or an over-marketed SaaS landing page. Avoid decorative sports-media tropes, oversized promotional layouts, glossy game-shop energy, and news-feed-first hierarchy.

## Design Principles

Make league state scannable before it is beautiful.

Keep actions close to the decisions they advance.

Use density intentionally so expert users can move quickly without hunting.

Treat simulation credibility as the brand; avoid hype unless the state itself earns it.

Preserve a consistent product vocabulary across tables, cards, forms, navigation, and status states.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

No custom accessibility requirements are currently defined. Default to production product standards: keyboard-friendly navigation and controls, visible focus states, readable contrast, semantic table structure where data is tabular, reduced-motion support for animation, and status cues that do not rely on color alone.
